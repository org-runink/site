## What Is a Carrier Scorecard and Why Do Most Shippers Still Lack One?

{{< direct-answer >}}
A carrier scorecard is a standardized performance report that evaluates each transportation partner against a consistent set of KPIs — on-time delivery, claims ratio, tender acceptance, billing accuracy, and more — scored, weighted, and reviewed on a recurring cadence to drive accountability and inform procurement decisions.
{{< /direct-answer >}}

If you manage 30, 50, or 100+ carriers, you already know the problem. The account manager at your third-largest LTL provider insists their service is excellent. Your dock supervisors disagree. Your AP team flags billing discrepancies every cycle. And your customer service group is fielding complaints about late deliveries on a lane you thought was covered. Everyone has an opinion. Nobody has data.

That gap — between perception and performance — is exactly what a carrier scorecard is designed to close. According to the Council of Supply Chain Management Professionals (CSCMP), fewer than 40% of mid-market shippers operate a formalized carrier evaluation program, even though carrier performance is the single largest variable in OTIF (on-time, in-full) outcomes. The rest rely on quarterly check-ins, relationship history, and the occasional escalation email.

The cost of this approach is invisible until it compounds. Underperforming carriers hold onto lanes they should lose. High-performing carriers receive no recognition — and no incentive to prioritize your freight when capacity tightens. Rate negotiations happen without performance context, leaving millions on the table. A scorecard does not fix every carrier problem, but it creates the foundation for every conversation that does.

## Which Metrics Belong on a Carrier Scorecard?

{{< direct-answer >}}
The essential carrier scorecard metrics include on-time pickup percentage, on-time delivery percentage, claims ratio, tender acceptance rate, EDI compliance, billing accuracy, transit time variability, and damage rate — each capturing a distinct dimension of carrier reliability, communication, and financial integrity.
{{< /direct-answer >}}

Not every metric matters equally, but every metric on this list earns its place by answering a specific operational question:

**On-time pickup % and on-time delivery % (OTD).** These are the foundational service metrics. OTD measures whether your carrier does the one thing you hired them to do — move freight from origin to destination within the agreed window. Industry benchmarks from Gartner's TMS research suggest that best-in-class shippers target 95%+ OTD across their carrier base, with top carriers consistently exceeding 97%.

**Tender acceptance rate.** When you tender a load, does the carrier accept it? A carrier with a 70% acceptance rate is not a reliable partner — they are a backup option masquerading as a primary. Low tender acceptance forces you into the spot market, where rates can spike 30-50% above contract. This metric is especially critical during peak season and capacity crunches.

**Claims ratio and damage rate.** Cargo claims are expensive, time-consuming, and a direct signal of handling quality. Track both the frequency (claims per 100 shipments) and the severity (average claim value). The American Trucking Associations reports that cargo claims remain one of the top five cost drivers for shippers managing FTL and LTL programs.

**Billing accuracy.** If your freight audit team is correcting 8-12% of invoices from a given carrier, that is not an accounting nuisance — it is a systemic data quality problem that inflates freight spend and erodes trust. Billing accuracy below 95% should trigger a formal review.

**EDI compliance.** Status updates, shipment milestones, proof of delivery — EDI compliance measures whether a carrier communicates reliably through your TMS. Poor EDI compliance creates visibility black holes that cascade into customer service failures and planning errors.

**Transit time variability.** Average transit time matters less than consistency. A carrier that delivers in 2.1 days on average but swings between 1.5 and 4 days is harder to plan around than one averaging 2.5 days with a tight standard deviation. MIT FreightLab research has consistently demonstrated that transit time variability is a stronger predictor of supply chain disruption than average transit time alone.

## How Should You Weight and Score Carrier Performance?

{{< direct-answer >}}
Not all KPIs are equal. Effective scorecards assign weighted scores that reflect each shipper's operational priorities — typically weighting OTD and tender acceptance highest, followed by claims and billing accuracy, with EDI compliance and transit variability as supporting metrics.
{{< /direct-answer >}}

A flat, unweighted scorecard treats EDI compliance the same as on-time delivery. That produces misleading results. A carrier scoring 99% on EDI but 88% on OTD should not rank above a carrier scoring 96% OTD with moderate EDI gaps. The weighting must reflect your network's actual pain points.

A common starting framework allocates weights as follows: OTD (30%), tender acceptance (20%), claims ratio (15%), billing accuracy (15%), transit time variability (10%), and EDI compliance (10%). But these weights are not universal. A shipper running a just-in-time automotive supply chain will weight transit variability far higher than a bulk commodities shipper. A retailer managing peak-season surge capacity will elevate tender acceptance above all else.

The scoring model itself should be simple enough for carrier account managers to understand. A 100-point scale works well — each carrier earns a composite score that maps to a performance tier: Preferred (90+), Approved (75-89), Probation (60-74), and Exit (below 60). These tiers should carry real consequences: preferred carriers receive first-right-of-refusal on new lanes and volume commitments; probation carriers receive a 90-day improvement plan with defined targets; exit-tier carriers are removed from the routing guide.

## What Is the Right Cadence for Scorecard Reviews?

{{< direct-answer >}}
Best-in-class shipper programs operate on a three-tier cadence: monthly scorecards for operational tracking, quarterly business reviews (QBRs) for strategic alignment, and annual performance evaluations tied to contract renewals and rate negotiations.
{{< /direct-answer >}}

Monthly scorecards are the operational heartbeat. They provide carrier account teams with a rolling performance snapshot — no surprises, no ambiguity. When a carrier's OTD drops from 96% to 91% in a given month, the scorecard captures it immediately, enabling a targeted conversation before the problem becomes a pattern.

Quarterly business reviews elevate the discussion from individual metrics to strategic alignment. QBRs are where you review lane-level performance trends, discuss capacity commitments, address systemic issues like billing disputes or claims patterns, and align on upcoming demand forecasts. This is also where you share your scorecard methodology transparently — carriers who understand exactly how they are measured will optimize toward those metrics.

Annual evaluations tie performance to procurement. When contract renewal season arrives, your scorecard data becomes your negotiating leverage. A carrier sitting at 93 composite with strong tender acceptance has earned rate stability. A carrier at 72 composite with declining OTD and rising claims has not — and your data makes that case objectively.

## How Do Scorecards Actually Change Carrier Behavior?

{{< direct-answer >}}
Carriers allocate capacity, assign drivers, and prioritize service based on which shippers measure performance and communicate expectations clearly. A structured scorecard program signals that your freight matters — and that accountability flows in both directions.
{{< /direct-answer >}}

This is the insight that transforms a scorecard from a reporting exercise into a strategic tool: carriers respond to measurement. When a carrier knows that their tender acceptance rate is tracked and that falling below 85% triggers a lane reallocation, they adjust dispatch priorities accordingly. When they know that billing errors above 5% result in a QBR escalation, their invoicing team tightens processes. Measurement creates incentives. Incentives drive behavior.

The reverse is equally true. Shippers who do not measure carrier performance signal that service quality is negotiable. During capacity crunches — the exact moments when carrier performance matters most — those shippers are the first to see loads rejected, deliveries delayed, and spot rates quoted at premiums.

This is also where automation becomes essential. Manually compiling scorecard data from TMS exports, ERP billing records, claims databases, and EDI logs is a full-time job — and the delay between data collection and scorecard delivery erodes the program's value. By the time a manually assembled scorecard lands in a carrier's inbox, the performance it reflects is six weeks old.

## Conclusion

{{< direct-answer >}}
Carrier scorecards are the most underutilized lever in transportation management. They convert subjective opinions into objective performance data, create accountability structures that improve service levels, and provide the analytical foundation for smarter procurement decisions.
{{< /direct-answer >}}

The shippers who win in competitive freight markets are not necessarily the ones moving the most volume. They are the ones who know — with precision — which carriers are earning their lanes and which are coasting on inertia. A structured scorecard program, backed by weighted KPIs, a defined review cadence, and real consequences for underperformance, is how you build that knowledge into your operation.

The question is not whether you should measure carrier performance. The question is whether you can afford the cost of not measuring it — in missed deliveries, inflated claims, wasted spot spend, and eroded customer confidence.
